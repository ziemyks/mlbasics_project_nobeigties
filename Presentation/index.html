The Challenge

Why Fraud Detection Is Hard

A new credit card company in the western US wants to protect its customers. They hired us to build a model that flags suspicious transactions — and the executive was clear:

"The model should err on the side of caution — it is better to flag a legitimate transaction than to miss real fraud."

This means our primary metric is Recall — out of all actual fraud cases, how many does the model catch?

The Accuracy Trap

Only 0.52% of transactions are fraud. A model that always says "not fraud" would be:

99.5%
accurate — but catches ZERO fraud

That's why accuracy is useless here. We measure what actually matters: Recall, F1-Score, and ROC-AUC.

Recall

Of all real fraud cases, what percentage did we catch? Like a security guard — one who catches 90% of shoplifters has 90% recall.

F1-Score

Balances Recall and Precision into a single number. The overall "grade" — avoids gaming one metric at the expense of the other.

ROC-AUC

How well the model ranks fraud higher than legitimate overall. Score from 0.5 (random guessing) to 1.0 (perfect separation).

Exploratory Data Analysis

What Does Fraud Look Like?

Before training any model, we explored the data to find patterns. These discoveries directly guided which features we engineered and which models we chose.

Fraud Rate by Hour of Day

Fraud peaks between 1–4 AM — criminals act while victims sleep.

Top Fraud Categories

Online categories (shopping_net, misc_net) have elevated fraud rates — no physical card check.

Transaction Amount

Fraud transactions have a significantly higher median amount than legitimate ones. Criminals go big.

Geographic Distance

Fraudulent merchants are much further from the cardholder's location — distance became one of our top features.

Correlations

The correlation heatmap confirmed that amount and category are the strongest individual signals, but the real power comes from combining them.

Feature Engineering

Turning Raw Data Into Fraud Signals

The raw dataset had timestamps and coordinates — but models can't learn from a datetime string. We created 6 new features that encode domain knowledge about how fraud works.

🕑

hour / day_of_week / month

Extracted from timestamp. Captures the 1–4 AM fraud peak and weekly/seasonal patterns discovered in EDA.

👤

age

Transaction date minus date of birth. Certain age groups are targeted more frequently by fraudsters.

📍

distance_km

Haversine formula: straight-line distance between cardholder and merchant coordinates. A $4,000 purchase 8,000 km away screams fraud.

📈

log_amt

ln(1 + amount) — compresses the extreme range ($1–$28,000+) and reduces outlier influence. Standard technique for skewed financial data.

Also Applied

  • One-Hot Encoding — converted category (14 values) and state (13 values) into binary columns
  • StandardScaler — rescaled numeric features to mean=0, std=1 (fitted on training data only to prevent leakage)
  • Dropped — merchant name, city, job, transaction ID (non-predictive or high-cardinality)

Result

15 raw columns became 39 engineered features — and the features we created ranked among the model's top predictors in Section 7.

Model Training

5 Algorithms, From Simple to Powerful

We trained 5 models of increasing complexity. All were tuned using 5-fold cross-validation with F1-Score as the optimisation metric. SMOTE was applied to the training set to balance classes at 50/50.

1

Logistic Regression

Linear boundary + sigmoid probability. Fast, interpretable — our baseline. Cannot capture feature interactions.

GridSearchCV · C, solver
2

Decision Tree

Learns yes/no questions: "Amount > $500? Hour > 22?" Non-linear, but a single tree overfits easily.

GridSearchCV · max_depth, criterion
3

Random Forest

Ensemble of many independent trees that vote together. Crowd wisdom reduces overfitting. Trees don't learn from each other's mistakes.

RandomizedSearchCV · n_estimators, depth
4

XGBoost

Gradient boosting — each new tree corrects the previous tree's errors. Like editors, each fixing what the last one missed.

RandomizedSearchCV · lr, depth, subsample
5

LightGBM

Microsoft's fast gradient boosting. Same idea as XGBoost but faster and more memory-efficient on large datasets. Our deployed model.

RandomizedSearchCV · leaves, lr, depth

F1-Score & ROC-AUC

Key takeaway: Accuracy barely moves (98.9%–99.9%) across all models — confirming it's useless on imbalanced data. The real story is in Recall and F1. Gradient boosting models (XGBoost, LightGBM) clearly dominate, catching up to 87.6% of all fraud cases.
